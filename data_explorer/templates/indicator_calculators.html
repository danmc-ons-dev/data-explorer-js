Heat- and cold-related mortality Indicator

This calculator returns the relative mortality risk attributable to non-optimal outdoor heat and cold for a given time series. This is estimated using a distributed lag non-linear model (DLNM). Full details of the methodology will be provided in the Heat and Cold framework documentation .

This calculator returns the following data:

  • Relative risk for the temperature-mortality association per disaggregated geography or aggregated across geographies.
  • Number of deaths attributable to non-optimal outdoor heat and cold per disaggregated geography or aggregated across geographies.
  • Deaths per 100,000 population attributable to non-optimal outdoor heat and cold per disaggregated geography or aggregated across geographies.

Heat and cold indicator tool instructions

  • Obtain and prepare data following the guidance in sections 2 and 3.2 of the methodology document
  • Upload your data (.csv) file
  • If disaggregating by geography, then also select a column to match the 'sub-geography' dropdown box
  • Select additional independent variables, if required
  • Choose the number of lag days (number of days after the exposure event to consider in the model)
  • Calculate your results, and interpret your outputs using guidance in section 4 of the methodology document

These maps show the rate of mortality per 100,000 population attributed to extreme cold (left) and hot days (right) . Extreme cold and hot days are days when the mean daily temperature is below the 2.5th (cold) and above the 97.5th percentile (hot) over the previous 5-15 years. The attributable rate of mortality is divided into quartiles; the value ranges for these quartiles are presented in the legend. Note: The threshold for extreme heat and cold varies among regions.

The above plot shows the temperature related relative risk of mortality (RR) in the data for . RR is the likelihood of an individual dying during or shortly after exposure to a given temperature with respect to the reference temperature. If one geography is used, he reference temperature is the mean temperature across the full time series. If multiple geographies are used and a meta-analysis is ran, the reference temperature is the minimum mortality temperature (MMT), where the RR is lowest. An RR of greater than 1 means there is an increase in the likelihood of an individual dying. The shaded area above and below the curve represent 95% confidence intervals. The optimal temperature range is the temperature range where RR is between and equal to 1 and 1.1.

This plot shows the rate of mortality per 100,000 population attributed to extreme cold (blue) and hot (red) days for . Extreme cold and hot days are days when the mean daily temperature is below the 2.5th (cold) and above the 97.5th (hot) percentile over the previous 5-15 years. Note: The threshold for extreme heat and cold varies among regions.

The above plot shows the number of deaths attributable to high-intensity heat (red) and cold (blue) in for . High-intensity hot and cold days are defined as days where the mean daily temperature is above the 97.5th or below the 2.5th percentiles for heat and cold, respectively. The percentiles are determined by the distribution of mean temperature observed in [geography] across all years in the time series.
