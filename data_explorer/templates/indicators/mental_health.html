Mental Health Indicator: Suicides attributable to extreme temperature

This tool estimates suicides attributable to extreme heat by applying a time-stratified case-crossover approach with a distributed lag non-linear model (DLNM) to your data.

Full details of the methodology and guidance on input data are provided in the Mental Health Framework Documentation

Important information:

Please upload your daily time series CSV. This should be prepared in accordance with the guidance in section 2.3 and 3.2 of the{{ link.external_link('https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.14050224', 'methodology documentation.') }}

Due to the nature of the health outcome, low counts of suicide are likely and therefore potentially susceptible to large uncertainty. Please interpret any outputs alongside the confidence intervals and model validation statistics.

This calculator returns the following data:

  • Model validation statistics
  • Relative risk: association between mean temperature and suicide
  • Attributable fraction: percentage of suicides attributable to extreme heat
  • Attributable number: number of suicides attributable to extreme heat
  • Attributable rate: suicides per 100,000 population attributable to extreme heat

Important information:

This tool utilises the baseline (default) model parameters only. These cannot be altered here but can be adjusted within the R package on GitHub.

The default model approach uses:

  • A smooth temperature-response curve (a quadratic b-spline) with key points (knots) evenly set at the 25th, 50th and 75th percentiles of daily temperature for the corresponding area.
  • Lag effects grouped into two periods: same day (lag 0) and the next two days (lag 1-2).
  • An extreme heat threshold of the top 2.5% (97.5th percentile) of daily temperatures for each corresponding area over the whole timeseries.

For more details see the {{ link.external_link('https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.14050224', 'methodology documentation.') }}

Relative risk of mortality
Important information:

The above plot shows the relative risk of suicide (RR) (and 95% confidence intervals) associated with mean temperature for the input data period.

The RR indicates the likelihood of an individual dying by suicide during, or shortly after, exposure to a certain temperature. When a temperature has a relative risk of one, this means there is neither an increase nor a decrease in the likelihood of the individual dying during, or shortly after, exposure to that temperature, compared with a reference temperature. In this case, the reference temperature is where the suicide risk is lowest between the first and fiftieth percentiles of daily mean temperature for the associated geography. A relative risk of 1.1 denotes a 10% increase in suicide risk. A relative risk of below one may suggest that, in the context of the model, extreme heat is associated with a reduction in risk (an inverse relationship).

Important information:

The above plot shows the estimated yearly number of deaths per 100,000 population that are attributable to extreme heat, that is, that would not have occurred if there had been no exposure to that heat.

The extreme heat threshold is defined as the 97.5th percentile of daily temperature over the input data period for the associated geography.
