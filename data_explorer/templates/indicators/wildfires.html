Health effects of extreme weather events - Wildfires

This tool estimates deaths attributable to wildfire-related PM2.5 by applying a standard time-stratified case-crossover approach with conditional Poisson regression model to your mortality data.

Full details of the methodology are provided in the Wildfires framework documentation

Important information:

Using user input data, this calculator returns the following results:

  • Relative risk of mortality for every 10 μg/m³ increase in wildfire‑related PM2.5.
  • Number of deaths attributable to wildfire‑related PM2.5.
  • Deaths per 100,000 people attributable to wildfire‑related PM2.5.

Important information:

The default model in this page estimates mortality attributable to wildfire related PM2.5 using a time-stratified case crossover design with a quasi-Poisson regression model.

Full details of the methodology are provided in the Wildfires framework documentation

Default (baseline) model specification:

  • The required exposure parameters are
    • Daily deaths counts
    • Daily mean temperature
    • Population (for the specific region)
    • Daily wildfire PM2.5 (This can be obtained from a{{ link.external_link('https://fmi.b2share.csc.fi/records/t3pww-vhh10', 'global open dataset') }}. Users will need a region’s geographic coordinates to extract daily wildfire related-PM2.5.)
  • Additional confounding variables such as daily relative humidity and daily wind speed may be included where data is available. However, these additional confounders are only supported in model available on{{ link.external_link('https://github.com/onssoschi/climatehealth', 'Github.') }}
  • The online indicator uses the following default settings. These can be adjusted in the full model available on Github.
    • Wildfire related-PM2.5 exposure lag : 3 days. This captures short-term delayed health effects of exposure.
    • Temperature lag: 1. Number of days of lag in the temperature variable from which to generate spline. The spline function allows for non-linear relationship between temperature and health outcome. Here the spline is applied at 0 (same-day temperature). And the spline uses 6 degrees of freedom.
  • Results calculated by this indicator are scaled to represent the relative risk associated with a 10µg/m3 increase in wildfire PM2.5 concentration.

Important information:

This above plot shows the relative risk of mortality (RR) (and 95% confidence intervals) associated with each 10μg/m3 increase in wildfire-related PM2.5 concentration for the input data period, for each lag period.

The RR is the likelihood of an individual dying during, or shortly after, exposure to a 10μg/m3 increase in wildfire-related PM2.5. When the RR is 1, this means there is neither an increase or decrease in the likelihood of the individual dying during, or shortly after, exposure to the increase in wildfire-related PM2.5 exposure. A RR of 1.10 denotes a 10% increase in the risk of dying.

Important information:

The estimated number of deaths that are attributable to wildfire-related PM2.5, that is, that would not have occurred if there had been no exposure to an increase in wildfire-related PM2.5 concentration.

Important information:

The estimated number of deaths per 100,000 population that are attributable to wildfire-related PM2.5, that is, that would not have occurred if there had been no exposure to an increase in wildfire-related PM2.5 concentration.
